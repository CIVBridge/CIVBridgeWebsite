Praxis I Design Summary: The Splartz

The Splartz is a dynamic air pump platform created to address the common issue of chair wobble in university study environments. Designed for square-legged chairs, it automatically adjusts to differences in leg height using internal elastic air pockets, thereby enhancing user comfort and focus without requiring permanent installation. The platform can support up to 150 kg and correct unevenness ranging from 1.25 mm to 12 mm, achieving a 98% correction rate during testing. Its compact size and adaptability allow for easy deployment across campus study spaces, promoting stability, user safety, and a more inclusive seating experience for all students.

Integrating the NSPIRE safety standard (Figure 1) during the framing stage of our engineering design process helped me recognize how technical guidelines enhance both user safety and design credibility. NSPIRE defines hazards like sharp edges and puncture risks that may endanger users‚Äîespecially in shared or institutional spaces. These criteria became foundational in how we structured our requirements, particularly by explicitly stating that no sharp corners or exposed structural elements could be present in the final product. This helped reframe our understanding of what makes a solution "complete"‚Äînot just functionally effective, but safe and inclusive.

Throughout divergence, NSPIRE helped us filter our brainstorming: we removed or reworked ideas that might violate its constraints. When converging, it became an evaluation lens, pushing us toward designs with enclosed geometries, rounded forms, and smooth external contours. In the representation phase, NSPIRE served as a justification tool‚Äîwe used references like compliant glides (Figure 2) to defend our decisions to both peers and instructors. Ultimately, it allowed us to validate our prototype with confidence that it aligned with real-world benchmarks.

Initially, I viewed standards as limitations‚Äîexternal impositions that restricted creative freedom. But applying NSPIRE early taught me that standards can instead anchor creativity in meaningful purpose. Rather than curbing our innovation, they enhanced it by creating clarity and intent. One challenge we faced was that interpreting general language (e.g., ‚Äúsharp edge‚Äù) required contextual judgment, which took iteration and peer feedback to resolve. Still, NSPIRE proved to be a powerful CTMF, shifting our focus from "how can we solve this problem?" to "how can we solve it safely and responsibly?"

CTMF 2 ‚Äì Diverging with the Lotus Blossom Method

During our divergence phase, we used the Lotus Blossom method (Figure 3) to expand a core problem‚Äîstabilizing wobbly chairs‚Äîinto eight broad categories of functionality, such as adjustability, material, footprint, and modularity. For each category, we generated specific concepts, like foldable platforms, magnetic locks, or rubber traction pads. This process unlocked idea generation that might otherwise have remained dormant, especially since it encouraged team members to work on different branches simultaneously, reducing groupthink and bottlenecks.

The Lotus method also supported framing by showing us which design considerations we were overlooking. Categories like ‚ÄúResisting the Load‚Äù or ‚ÄúUniversal Compatibility‚Äù became new requirement areas in our updated design brief. During convergence, the visual structure of the Lotus board helped us identify overlapping concepts, such as ‚Äúminimalist‚Äù designs that also reduced sharp edges, strengthening both safety and user experience. As we moved into representation, we used the filled-in board as a deliverable to explain our creative process to instructors and clients.

Critics may argue that group ideation models like Lotus can lead to idea overload or disjointed thinking. However, our team‚Äôs structured facilitation‚Äîtimeboxing categories and building off each other's ideas‚Äîtransformed the tool into a platform for real synergy. We created far more well-rounded, diverse options than we could have as individuals. In fact, some of our most promising concepts (like the detachable air-based platform) were the result of combining insights across different petals of the Lotus model.

Ultimately, this CTMF proved that design is not just about generating a solution, but building an ecosystem of possibilities. The Lotus method helped us balance depth with breadth, and it turned our divergence into a creative yet strategic process.
